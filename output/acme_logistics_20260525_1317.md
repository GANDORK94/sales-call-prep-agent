## 1. Company Overview

Acme Logistics is a mid-market third-party logistics provider operating in the Midwest, likely serving regional manufacturers, retailers, or distributors who need warehousing, fulfillment, and freight management. As a 3PL, they compete by offering flexible capacity and service coverage without the overhead of a captive private fleet. Their recent expansion into last-mile delivery signals both growth ambition and a willingness to take on more complex, customer-facing operations. This puts them in a transitional moment where operational efficiency directly affects their ability to compete with larger national carriers and tech-forward delivery networks.

## 2. Likely Pain Points

**Rising fuel and variable transportation costs**
Last-mile delivery is margin-thin, and fuel volatility hits 3PLs especially hard when routes are not optimized.
*Why it matters: Uncontrolled fuel spend erodes profitability on contracts that were priced before costs escalated.*

**Missed or late delivery windows damaging customer relationships**
As they move into last-mile, on-time delivery becomes a visible, customer-facing metric they may not have had to manage closely before.
*Why it matters: One bad delivery experience in last-mile can cost a client relationship that took years to build.*

**Dispatcher and driver inefficiency during rapid expansion**
Scaling a new delivery operation often means manual routing, over-reliance on driver judgment, and inconsistent execution.
*Why it matters: Inefficiency at this stage can permanently set a bad operational baseline as volume grows.*

**Difficulty proving ROI and performance to shippers**
Mid-market 3PLs often struggle to give clients real-time visibility into delivery performance, which limits their ability to retain and upsell accounts.
*Why it matters: Without clean delivery data, the VP of Ops cannot make the case internally or externally that the last-mile expansion is working.*

**Pressure to scale without proportionally scaling headcount**
Growth through last-mile means more routes, more drivers, and more coordination without necessarily having the team to manage it manually.
*Why it matters: The VP of Ops needs leverage, and technology is often the only path to scaling operations without hiring faster than revenue supports.*

## 3. Discovery Questions

1. "How are your teams currently building and managing routes for last-mile deliveries, and where does that process tend to break down?"

2. "When you look at your on-time delivery performance over the last quarter, what has been the biggest driver of missed windows?"

3. "What does the cost structure for your last-mile operation look like today, and where are you feeling the most pressure to improve margins?"

4. "As you expand last-mile capacity, what does success look like operationally in the next 12 months, and what is the biggest obstacle standing between you and that?"

5. "How are you currently measuring driver and route efficiency, and how confident are you in that data when you're making decisions?"

## 5. Pre-Call Briefing

**Anchor the conversation here:** Acme just entered last-mile delivery. That transition is where operational gaps show up fastest and cost the most. Start there and let everything else follow.

**Definitely ask these two questions:**
1. "How are you currently building and managing routes for last-mile, and where does that process break down?"
2. "Where are you feeling the most margin pressure in the last-mile operation right now?"

**Be ready for this objection:** "We're still early in our last-mile buildout, so it's not the right time to evaluate new tools." Respond by reframing: early-stage is actually the best time to set the right operational baseline, before bad habits and manual workarounds become entrenched.

**One additional risk to watch for:** The VP of Ops may not own the technology budget. Confirm early who else would be involved in evaluating a solution like this, so you are not building a deal on a single stakeholder.
